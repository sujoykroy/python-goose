Opposition pushes to reveal Turkish govt’s use of public resources to dominate media

Zülfikar Doğan
2019-05-24

A motion by Turkey’s main opposition party to investigate the use of public funding in the media sector is likely to reveal the details of how President Recep Tayyip Erdoğan's built his own partisan media empire and financed his close political circle.

Turkey's main opposition Republican People's Party (CHP) Istanbul deputy Erdoğan Toprak on May 22 has proposed a parliamentary inquiry into the siphoning of public resources to fund pro-government media.

"It is essential for the public to know what the standards for preference and advertising policy were regarding the transfer of public resources to the media through advertisements, what criteria were followed for the distribution of funding (to media outlets), and for the facts on these matters to be revealed," said Toprak in his proposal.

On May 28, the Grand National Assembly of Turkey (TBMM) will debate Toprak’s proposal. It is likely to be rejected by votes of Erdoğan's ruling Justice and Development Party (AKP) and its far-right ultra-nationalist ally, Nationalist Movement Party.

But regardless of that, the debates and discussion on the parliamentary inquiry will probably reveal details of how Erdoğan and his political elites took control of Turkey’s media, using public resources and manipulating public opinion for his circle’s political and financial gain.

President Erdoğan made his most significant move to seize the media in March 2018, when Turkey's largest and most influential media group, Doğan Media Holding, was sold to a pro-government Turkish conglomerate Demirören Group for $1 billion.

In addition to daily newspapers, Hürriyet and Posta, television channels CNN Turk and Kanal D as well as the digital broadcasting platform D Smart, and many magazines and radio stations were transferred to Yıldırım Demirören, a devoted Erdoğan supporter. Previously, Demirören also bought two newspapers of the Doğan Group, Milliyet and Vatan which were reporting on a social-democratic line.

The sale transferred ownership of high circulation daily newspapers Hürriyet and Posta, popular television channels CNN Türk and Kanal D, the digital broadcasting platform D Smart and a slew of magazines and radio stations to Yıldırım Demirören, a devoted supporter of Erdoğan.

Demirören’s purchase swiftly spelled the end for any independence in these outlets’ editorial line, just as his previous purchases of newspapers Milliyet and Vatan had turned the newspapers from social democratic to pro-AKP government vehicles almost overnight.

The takeover of respected media institutions by Erdoğan’s supporters has been facilitated by the AKP government since its first days in power, and was greatly helped by an economic crisis in 2001 that left several of the country’s largest holding companies, and the media outlets the owned, bankrupt.

These holding companies and banks were handed over to the government-controlled Savings Deposit Insurance Fund of Turkey (TMSF) after the crash, along with their media assets.

The list of those assets reads like a directory of the country’s largest and most established media firms. Star newspaper, Star TV, Kral TV, Digiturk, Show TV, Sky TV, Cine5, Akşam, Sabah and Takvim newspapers, ATV, A Haber TV…

Suddenly, the TMSF had turned into Turkey's largest media group – possibly the world’s. With the AKP’s ascent to power in 2002, this media presence in the TMSF created an extraordinary opportunity for Erdoğan's project of establishing his own media and silencing the opposition media.

A significant part of the TMSF-owned media, newspapers and televisions that were put up for sale without tender were sold to business people and holdings in the immediate vicinity of Erdoğan. Public banks such as Ziraat Bank, Vakıfbank and Halkbank financed the handover of the media by giving credit to companies and bosses close to the government.

Public banks were also active in the sale of Doğan Media to Demirören. For this sale, Ziraat Bank secured a 10-year loan of $1 billion to Demirören for three years.

Ethem Sancak, another member of Erdoğan's close political circle, became the new boss of the TMSF-seized Star, Akşam, Güneş newspapers and television channels Star TV, Sky and Türk TV.

The TMSF also sold BMC, one of the largest commercial vehicle manufacturers in Turkey, to Sancak after its former owner Turkish billionaire Mehmet Emin Karamehmet ran into financial trouble during the banking crisis of 2001.

The links between BMC’s owner Sancak and Erdoğan’s AKP are well known. Sancak, now himself a member of the AKP’s executive board, has seen his businesses rapidly grow since the party came to power in 2002.

This has doubtless been helped by those companies’ success in securing government projects, such as BMC’s involvement in the $4 billion project to build Turkey’s national tank, Altay.

When the state-run Etibank went bankrupt in 2002, the TMSF also seized TV channel ATV and newspapers Sabah and Takvim. These were sold to Çalık Group owner Ahmet Çalık, another businessman known for his close ties to Erdoğan. Public banks, and help from investors from Qatar, provided the funds for the purchase.

Çalık later withdrew from the media sector after a probe on December 2013 into alleged corruption by Erdoğan and his inner circle. He delivered Sabah and ATV to Kalyon Group, another conglomerate favoured by the AKP government that has undertaken mega construction projects including the third airport in Istanbul.

The latest step towards full control over the media followed a coup attempt on July 15, 2016, which the Turkish government blames on the Gülen religious movement.

The companies belonging to alleged members of Gülen movement were confiscated and handed over to the TMSF, 955 companies and their advertising budgets were placed in the hands of government-appointed administrators. One of the first moves was to fire journalists critical of the AKP.

CHP politician Toprak's motion says that Erdoğan and his inner political circle have used their media companies and the public budget to shut down the opposing voices and imposed them "economic pressure" by cutting back their advertising resources to sustain their budgets.

Toprak also said some public institutions and companies like Turkish Airlines and Anadolu Jet have been buying thousands of copies pro-government newspapers to boost their circulations, helping them to make a profit from advertising.

Even though Erdoğan's party and the MHP will doubtless move to block Toprak's proposed enquiry on May 28, the parliamentary debate will at least bring these details, and more, into the open.
